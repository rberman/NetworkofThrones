Network Science

Connections are what networks are about. Cities are connected by roads and airline routes. Flora and fauna are bound together in a food web. Countries are involved in trading relationships. But not all networks are physical: the World Wide Web is a virtual network of information. Your friends and acquaintances form a social network. Likewise, characters in a book are enmeshed in a network of encounters and interactions.

More Information

This brings us to our mathematical definition of a network. A network models a set of objects where some pairs are related in a specified way. We refer to these objects as the vertices of the network. When we visualize a network, its vertices are drawn as points (dots, circles). The network relationships are modeled by edges that connect pairs of vertices. These edges are drawn as lines or arcs that connect pairs of vertices.

As we know from experience, not all relationships are equal: you are more closely related to your friends than to your acquaintances, even though you “know” both. Therefore, we assign a positive weight to each edge in order to indicate the strength of the relationship. Sometimes researchers look at directed edges to represent a one-way relationship.

Just as the edges of a network have attributes, the vertices of a network can have inherent characteristics. However, vertices gain additional qualities depending on their place and value in the network. For example, we can group vertices into communities, based on how frequently they interact with one another. We can also identify how important a particular vertex is to the network: Does it have a lot of connections? It is uniquely positioned to help to connect other pairs of vertices? Broadly speaking, these kinds of importance are called centrality measures, and we will explore multiple ways to “be central” in a given network.
